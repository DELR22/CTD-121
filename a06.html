Section 01

Introduction & Learning Outcomes

In Assignments 04 and 05, you worked forward—drawing a floor plan, sizing its elements against human measurements, understanding space through ergonomics and anthropometrics, and building a physical 1/8″ scale model. Here you work backward. This is called Reverse Engineering.

Using the same floor plan from Assignment 05, your task is to peel back the layers of the design to discover the hidden spatial logic underneath. By tracing the plan, you will learn the graphic code of interior design. By abstracting it into boxes, colors, and bubbles, you will learn how designers solved the spatial puzzle. By encoding those relationships into a matrix, you will translate spatial intuition into data.

Finally, by assembling all the steps into a freestanding object, you will reconstruct the design process as a coherent narrative—from the most abstract diagram to the most concrete form.

Reverse Engineering

Working backward is not undoing—it is a method of discovery. Every trace layer, every bubble, every matrix cell is an act of interpretation that reveals a designer’s decision.

The sequence of panels you assemble at the end should tell that story: from pure spatial data to a fully realized floor plan and physical model.

Bloom’s Taxonomy (2001)

  1. Understand how the floor plan distributes area across functional zones by abstracting it into a Block Plan.
  2. Analyze spatial relationships between rooms and spaces by evaluating the type, quality, and logic of adjacencies encoded in the plan.
  3. Evaluate the designer’s spatial decisions by interpreting the Bubble Diagram and Matrix as evidence.
  4. Create a freestanding Synthesis Object that sequences the design process from abstract to concrete in a coherent volumetric display.

CIDA / NASAD Standards

  • Design Process: Demonstrate the ability to apply design process methodologies, including precedent analysis and spatial diagramming, to understand built environments.
  • Interior Building Systems & Integration: Identify and interpret the spatial organization and functional logic of an existing floor plan.
  • Building & Space Analysis: Analyze the functional relationships between spaces through graphic abstraction tools, including block plans, bubble diagrams, and adjacency matrices.
  • Communication: Communicate spatial analysis through disciplined hand drawing, graphic notation, and the physical assembly of a display object.

Section 03

Instructions

Step 1 — The Analytic Trace

Objective: Understand the standard graphic language of architecture and develop discipline with line weights.

Before you begin, use your 1/8″ scale model from Assignment 05 to read the space—the rooms, their proximity, their relationships, and their boundaries. Analyze which boundaries are physical (walls) and which are abstract (open space defined by furniture), and consider how this shapes the spatial adjacencies.

  1. Print Deconstruction Format 1 on 11″×8.5″ paper and paste it onto a foam board.
  2. Cut three pieces of tracing paper matching the dimensions of the three rectangles printed on the format. Use your ruler and T-square to guarantee rigor and precision throughout.
  3. Trace the floor plan three times on separate sheets of tracing paper, one layer per sheet, following the line weights and conventions below.

Three Trace Layers

Layer 1 — 0.9mm Cut Partitions. Trace the walls. A floor plan is a horizontal section cut through a building viewed from above. Walls must be hatched with diagonal lines or filled with rigor and precision. Avoid hairy lines and hatching that overruns the wall boundary.
Layer 2 — 0.7mm Openings. Trace all doors, door swings, and windows.
Layer 3 — 0.5mm Details. Trace furniture, plumbing fixtures, cabinetry, text, and floor textures.
All three layers must overlay to reconstruct the complete plan. Paste each tracing paper layer onto the corresponding rectangle indicated on Format 1.

Step 2 — The Block Plan

Objective: Understand how an area program is distributed into defined zones.

Before you begin, use your 1/8″ scale model again to think about the activities that take place in each space—not just their boundaries, but their function.

  1. Print Deconstruction Format 2 on 11″×8.5″ paper and paste it onto a foam board.
  2. Cut three pieces of tracing paper matching the dimensions of the three rectangles printed on the format.
  3. Work through the three rectangles in sequence as described below.

Rectangle 1 — The Labeled Block Plan

Place the first tracing paper rectangle over the floor plan. Trace the boundaries of every space—remember that boundaries can be physical (a wall) or abstract (an open area divided by furniture). The result is a series of boxes side by side. Small spaces such as closets and pantries should be included as separate boxes, but are not required to be tagged.

For every major space, place a Room Tag inside its box:

NAME OF THE SPACEKITCHEN
DIMENSIONS in Feet12′ × 8′
AREA in Square Feet96 Sq. Ft.

Measure the length and width of each major space from the 1/16″ drawings in the Deconstruction Format, calculate the area in square feet, name the space, and record all three in the tag. Paste this layer onto the rectangle indicated on Format 2.

Rectangle 2 — The Colored Block Plan

Place the second tracing paper rectangle over the Labeled Block Plan. Trace only the major spaces—absorb minor dependencies, like closets and pantries, into the general block of the room they serve. Write the name of each space and fill each with a distinct color. The colors you choose here carry forward into Rectangle 3. Paste this layer onto the rectangle indicated on Format 2.

Rectangle 3 — The Bubble Diagram

Place the third tracing paper rectangle over the Colored Block Plan. Draw loose circles (bubbles) centered over each zone, using the same names and colors from Rectangle 2. Connect bubbles with the line types below to describe the relationships you observe in the plan. Mark the entry point of the plan. Paste this layer onto the rectangle indicated on Format 2.

Connection Line Types

Solid + Arrow Physical opening—door or archway.
Dashed Line Visual connection—open plan, pass-through, sightline.
Zig-Zag Line Sound barrier—acoustic separation between quiet and noisy zones.

Step 3 — The Adjacency Matrix

Objective: Create a graphic abstraction of the spatial and functional relationships between spaces.

Before you begin, use your 1/8″ scale model once more to analyze which boundaries are physical and which are abstract, and how this defines the spatial adjacencies between rooms.

  1. Print Deconstruction Format 3 on 11″×8.5″ paper and paste it onto a foam board.
  2. Work through the four stages below in sequence.

Four Stages

Stage 1 Paste or tape tracing paper over the floor plan on Format 3. Draw boxes over each space and name them to match the room names you will use in your Adjacency Matrix grid.
Stage 2 For each space, consider and list the activities that occur within it.
Stage 3 Based on those activities, analyze the type of adjacency relationship between each pair of spaces.
Stage 4 Fill every intersecting cell of the Adjacency Matrix grid using the three-symbol key below. Every cell must be filled. No blanks. Your symbol choices should be consistent with the Bubble Diagram and with what you observe in the floor plan.

Symbol Key

● Primary Adjacency—these spaces must be next to each other.
○ Secondary Adjacency—these spaces should be close or convenient.
– No Relation—proximity does not matter.
For further guidance on completing the Adjacency Matrix, watch the explanation video: youtu.be/fYFwns2cZ30

Step 4 — The Synthesis Object

Objective: Create a narrative within a volumetric composition that reconstructs and explains the design process.

Connect all four foam boards—including the 1/8″ scale model from Assignment 05—using masking-tape hinges on the reverse side so they fold into a freestanding volumetric display structure.

Structure Requirements

Stand independently—the structure may not lean against a wall or another object.

Read in sequence—panels must be arranged so a viewer can follow the design process from most abstract to most concrete.

The Synthesis Object is not decoration—it is an argument. The sequence of panels should tell the story of how a designer moves from pure spatial data to a fully realized floor plan and physical model.

Section 04

Evaluation Rubric

Criterion Excellent Proficient Developing
Graphic Precision & Line Weight Discipline 40 pts
35–40 pts
All three trace layers are clearly distinguishable by pencil weight. Walls are uniformly hatched or filled with no overruns. Lines are controlled, straight, and precise. All three layers overlay correctly to reconstruct the complete plan.
22–34 pts
Pencil weights are used but inconsistently—some layers are difficult to distinguish. Hatching is present but uneven in places, with minor overruns. Lines are mostly straight but occasionally waver. Layers overlay with minor misregistration.
0–21 pts
Little or no differentiation between line weights. Walls are not hatched or are hatched carelessly, with significant overruns. Lines are imprecise without the use of a ruler. Layers do not register or are incomplete.
Spatial Abstraction — Block Plan & Bubble Diagram 70 pts
60–70 pts
The block plan accurately captures the spatial distribution of the floor plan, with major spaces clearly defined, labeled, and tagged. The color system is consistent and carries legibly into the bubble diagram. Spatial relationships are correctly identified and meaningfully encoded using all three connection line types.
40–59 pts
The spatial distribution is mostly legible, but some spaces are imprecisely defined or incompletely tagged. The color system is present but inconsistent. The bubble diagram shows relationships, but the line types are misapplied.
0–39 pts
The spatial distribution does not clearly correspond to the floor plan. Tags are largely absent or incorrect. Color is missing or applied arbitrarily. The bubble diagram is incomplete or contradicts the block plan.
Adjacency Matrix — Completeness & Logic 40 pts
35–40 pts
All three symbols are used and applied consistently with the Bubble Diagram and the observed floor plan. Relationship assignments are spatially logical. No cells are left blank.
22–34 pts
At least two symbol types are used. Some relationships are logical, but others contradict the Bubble Diagram or lack spatial justification. A few cells are blank or carry the wrong symbol.
0–21 pts
The grid is incomplete. Only one symbol type is used. Relationships do not correspond to the Bubble Diagram or the observed floor plan.
Synthesis Object — Sequence & Craft 50 pts
44–50 pts
The sequence reads clearly from the adjacency matrix to the physical model. Panels are clean, flat, and presented with care. The object functions as a coherent argument about the design process.
28–43 pts
The structure stands but is unstable or requires minor support. The sequence is mostly legible, but one panel is out of order, or the transition between abstract and concrete is unclear. Some panels show handling damage or careless assembly.
0–27 pts
The structure does not stand independently. Panels are connected incorrectly or in an incoherent order. The sequence does not communicate the design process. Assembly is careless or incomplete.
