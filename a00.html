Section 01

Introduction

Design does not only happen in the studio during class hours. Inspiration strikes in a coffee shop or at the park; a technical detail reveals itself in a library chair; a lighting condition appears in a hallway.

This semester-long assignment is designed to build the most important habit of a professional designer: Continuous Observation. You are required to carry your sketchbook with you everywhere. It is your external hard drive. You will use it to record ideas, measure reality, and capture space.

Continuous Observation

This is an exercise in discipline, curiosity, and muscle memory. The sketchbook is not a place to draw only when assigned—it is a place to draw because you cannot help it.

Make drawing a habit. Do not leave these activities until the last minute.

Section 02

The Format — Back-to-Front

To distinguish your Formal Class Work from your Field Research, you will organize your sketchbook physically into two halves, read from opposite ends.

→
Front-to-Back
Reserved for regular Assignments (A01, A02, A03, etc.). Start on the first page and work forward, as in a normal notebook.
←
Back-to-Front
Reserved for Autonomous Sketching. Start on the very last page of your notebook and work backward toward the middle. This is the home for everything in this assignment.

Section 03

The Requirements

Over the course of the semester, you must produce a minimum of 20 high-quality entries in the back of your sketchbook, divided into four specific categories of five entries each.

Part 1

Conceptual Ideation

5 Entries

Goal: Capture fleeting ideas and moods.
Subject: Interior design concepts, furniture ideas, material combinations, or abstract thoughts.
Technique: Mixed Media. You are encouraged to use color (markers, watercolors), texture rubbings, or collage.
The Artifact Rule: If an idea strikes you while you are drawing on a napkin in a restaurant, keep the napkin. Paste it into your sketchbook. If you find a material sample or a ticket stub with a great color palette, tape it in. This section is for inspiration.
Part 2

Object Analysis

5 Entries

Goal: Understand how things are built and sized.
Subject: Furniture, millwork (cabinetry), or interesting design objects found in the real world (cafes, libraries, your home).
Technique: Pencil (Graphite).

The Views — For Each Object

  • Photograph. Take a single, well-taken, clear, and comprehensive photograph of each object, capturing its three-dimensional nature—form, joinery, and overall configuration. Print and paste it into your sketchbook.
  • Orthographic Views. Draw Top, Front, and Side views, as instructed in the Orthographic Projections Assignment.
  • The Data. Use a tape measure to record actual dimensions—seat height, leg thickness, backrest angle, or whatever dimensions define the object’s essential character. Include these dimensions in your orthogonal view drawings.
Note: Do not guess. Measure. Format: in a single page, include the photograph, Top, Front, and Side views with general measurements.
Part 3

Traced Floor Plans, Elevation & Perspective Combos

5 Entries

Goal: Learn from the Masters.
Task: The teacher will provide a list of reference works of architecture and interior design, each composed of a floor plan, an elevation or section, and an interior perspective. Print and paste five combos from the list and trace over them using tracing paper and accurate pencils to adjust for line widths.
Technique: Freehand-traced drawings. Focus on line weights and textures.
Labeling: Identify the building (e.g., “Villa Savoye”), location (e.g., “Poissy, France”), and designer (e.g., “Le Corbusier”).
Part 4

Spatial Renderings

5 Entries

Goal: Capture volume, light, and perspective.
Subject: Interesting spaces found within the University of Alabama campus—interiors (lobbies, stairwells, classrooms) or exteriors (plazas, colonnades, porticos).
Technique: Freehand Perspective. Focus on depth and shadow.
Labeling: Identify the building and the specific location (e.g., “Gorgas Library, Main Reading Room”).

Section 05

Evaluation Rubric

Criteria Excellent (90–100) Proficient (70–89) Developing (0–69)
Part 1 — Conceptual Ideation75 pts
90–100%
The five ideas demonstrate genuine creative restlessness. The student uses the sketchbook as a thinking tool. Mixed media is employed intentionally: color, texture, and collage serve the idea rather than decorate the page. Entries read as temporally distributed and authentically spontaneous.
70–89%
Ideation entries are present and show engagement with the assignment’s spirit. Mixed media appears limited. The student is building the habit of capturing ideas outside the studio, though entries do not yet reflect sustained commitment.
0–69%
Entries are present but function as obligatory sketches rather than genuine ideation. Media use is limited. The work reads as retroactive rather than spontaneous.
Part 2 — Object Analysis75 pts
90–100%
Each object is documented with a single, well-composed photograph that conveys its three-dimensional form, construction logic, and material character. Orthographic views are proportionally sound and internally consistent with the photographic evidence. Tape-measure dimensions are recorded with specificity.
70–89%
Photographs are present and adequate. Orthographic views demonstrate understanding of the projection system, though inconsistencies in proportions or missing dimensions indicate incomplete observational rigor.
0–69%
Photographs are absent, unclear, or fail to convey the object’s form. Orthographic views are loosely rendered without dimensional grounding. Dimensions are inaccurate or missing entirely.
Part 3 — Traced Masters75 pts
90–100%
Each traced combo demonstrates that the student read the drawings before tracing them. Line weights are differentiated with purpose: cut lines, seen lines, and surface textures are graphically distinguished. Labeling is complete and accurate.
70–89%
Traced drawings are present and legible. Some line weight differentiation is evident, though it may be inconsistent across the five entries. Labeling is mostly complete.
0–69%
Traced drawings are present but treated as copying exercises rather than analytical acts. Line weights are uniform or ignored. Labeling is incomplete or absent.
Part 4 — Spatial Rendering75 pts
90–100%
The five campus spaces are rendered with genuine spatial intelligence. Perspective is coherent. The student sought out challenging, architecturally rich spaces rather than defaulting to convenient or visually simple locations. Each entry is labeled with the building and the specific location.
70–89%
Spatial renderings demonstrate an emerging understanding of perspective and depth. The choice of spaces shows some ambition, though the student may gravitate toward simpler compositions. Labeling is present.
0–69%
Perspective is fundamentally inconsistent or absent. Drawings are flat. Spaces chosen are generic or visually undifferentiated. Labeling is incomplete or missing.
Total 300 pts  across four parts of 75 pts each
