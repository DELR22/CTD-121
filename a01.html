Form, Shape,

Section 01

General Description & Student Learning Outcomes

Through structured, instrument-based drafting exercises, students develop precision, graphic discipline, and an early understanding of how designers translate abstract ideas into measurable, proportional representations of space.

The assignment is organized in two parts. Part 1 establishes the foundational relationship between form and shape through the construction of grids and emergent geometric patterns. Part 2 extends this foundation into scale literacy, requiring students to redraw the same structures at progressively smaller architectural scales.

Together, both parts introduce the conventions of technical drawing as a professional design language — one governed by vocabulary, grammar, and the proportional discipline of the architect’s scale.

Recording the making process is as important as the final result. Students are expected to photograph or scan their work at each meaningful stage of progress and upload these records to the course’s collaborative board. In design practice, process documentation is evidence of thinking, decision-making, and craft development. Learning to record work in progress is itself a professional skill introduced here.

Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (2001)

  • Remember The distinction between form and shape as defined in design theory, identifying form as the intrinsic nature and purpose of a space and shape as its physical and geometric realization.
  • Understand How scale functions as a proportional tool that relates a drawn representation to the human body and physical space.
  • Apply Manual drafting instruments — including the architect’s scale, T-square, triangle rulers, and compass — to construct grids, margins, and geometric patterns with accuracy and consistency.
  • Apply Professional line weight conventions by selecting appropriate drawing instruments for structural versus pattern elements across multiple drawing scales.
  • Analyze How the same underlying geometric structure reads differently across the 1″=1′-0″, 1/2″, 1/4″, and 1/8″ scales, recognizing how scaling affects visual weight, proportion, and legibility.
  • Create A visual record of the making process by documenting progressive stages of work through photographs or scans, organized and shared on the course collaborative board.

CIDA / NASAD Framework

  • Demonstrate Foundational technical drawing proficiency by constructing grids, margins, and geometric patterns using professional drafting instruments in accordance with established graphic conventions.
  • Apply Scale as a communicative tool by accurately redrawing the same pattern structures at four distinct architectural scales, reflecting the proportional relationship between drawn representation and real-world space.
  • Differentiate Graphic line weight and instrument selection across drawing scales, demonstrating an understanding of how line hierarchy functions in design communication.
  • Articulate The conceptual relationship between form and shape as a foundational framework, recognizing form as an underlying essential structure and shape as its emergent physical expression.
  • Produce Technical drawings that meet professional graphic standards, including precision of line, consistency of hatch pattern, and legibility across multiple scales on a single drawing surface.
  • Document And communicate the design process by photographing or scanning progressive stages of work and contributing them to the shared course collaborative board, reflecting professional standards for process transparency and collaborative critique.

Part 1 Form and Shape

When we design space, we must address the inherent tension between Form and Shape. Form is the “what” — it represents the intrinsic nature, purpose, and structural integrity of a space. Form exists in the mind and doesn’t depend on shape, dimensions, or specific materials.

Shape, subsequently, is the “how” — it refers to the actual physical boundaries, geometry, and materials (like circles, squares, or triangles) chosen to bring the Form forth.

In this exercise, we will define the Form by drawing grids that support emergent patterns that are later defined by selectively retracing over this underlying structure.

Instructions

  1. Follow the instruction videos to complete the assignment. The videos will be available through Blackboard.
  2. A total of three (3) patterns and their underlying structure must be drawn on 11″ × 8.5″ foam boards — one per board.
  3. All drawings must be done using drawing instruments exclusively:
    • Architect’s scale
    • 45° and 30° triangle rulers
    • T-Square ruler
    • Compass
  4. Margins, grids, and patterns must be drawn at 1″=1′-0″ scale.
  5. Margins and grids must be drawn using the 0.5mm mechanical pencil.
  6. Patterns must be drawn using the 2mm lead holder. Each pattern must be defined by crisp and clean dark lines and filled with a 45° diagonal hatch.
  7. Photograph or scan each board after the grid structure is complete and before patterns are drawn. Upload this image to the course Miro board.
  8. Photograph or scan each board once the pattern and hatch are complete. Upload this image to the course Miro board. Process uploads made only at submission will not meet this requirement.

02
Part 2 Form, Shape, and Scale

Technical drawing is a language. It has vocabulary made from conventions and symbols, and grammar composed of geometric structure and order. This exercise focuses particularly on the order defined by scale.

As Interior Designers, we use scale to refer to the proportional relationship between a physical space and the human body — it serves as a mathematical tool to “zoom in” or “zoom out” to include relevant information in technical drawings.

To introduce this concept, we will use the margins, grids, and patterns from Part 1 and redraw all three underlying structures and corresponding patterns in three different smaller scales.

Instructions

  1. Follow the instruction videos to complete the assignment. The videos will be available through Blackboard.
  2. A total of nine (9) patterns and their underlying structure must be drawn on the 11″ × 17″ foam board. Each underlying structure and pattern must be redrawn in a different scale, as the videos instruct.
  3. All drawings must be done using drawing instruments exclusively:
    • Architect’s scale
    • 45° and 30° triangle rulers
    • T-Square ruler
    • Compass
  4. Margins, grids, and patterns must be drawn to the following scales:
    • 1/2″
    • 1/4″
    • 1/8″
  5. Margins and grids must be drawn using the 0.5mm mechanical pencil.
  6. Patterns must be drawn with the following instrument per scale:
    • 1/2″ — 2mm lead holder
    • 1/4″ — 0.9mm mechanical pencil
    • 1/8″ — 0.7mm mechanical pencil
  7. Each pattern must be defined by crisp and clean dark lines and filled with a 45° diagonal hatch, using the corresponding pencil.
  8. Photograph or scan the 11″ × 17″ board after all underlying grid structures are complete and before any patterns are drawn. Upload this image to the course Miro board.
  9. Photograph or scan the completed board once all nine patterns and hatches are finished. Upload this image to the course Miro board. Process uploads made only at submission will not meet this requirement.

Section 04

Evaluation Rubric

The assignment is evaluated across five criteria totaling 300 points. Each criterion is scored within one of three performance levels. Pattern Completeness and Scale Accuracy carry greater weight as the core elements of the exercise.

Criterion Excellent Proficient Developing
1. Instrument Use & Drawing Technique 30 pts total
21 – 30 pts
All instruments used correctly throughout. Lines are precise, angles accurate, and curves clean across all four boards with no freehand marks.
11 – 20 pts
Instruments used correctly in most instances. Minor inaccuracies in line straightness, angle precision, or curve quality are present but do not significantly affect overall legibility.
0 – 10 pts
Instrument use is inconsistent or incorrect in multiple instances. Freehand marks, crooked lines, or imprecise angles are evident across one or more boards.
2. Line Weight & Instrument Differentiation 30 pts total
21 – 30 pts
Line weight hierarchy is clear and consistent. The 0.5mm pencil defines all margins and grids; the 2mm, 0.9mm, and 0.7mm tools are correctly applied to patterns at their respective scales across all nine drawings in Part 2.
11 – 20 pts
Line weight differentiation is mostly correct. One or two instances of incorrect tool assignment or inconsistent weight are present but the overall hierarchy remains legible.
0 – 10 pts
Line weight differentiation is unclear or incorrect in multiple drawings. The structural and pattern layers are difficult to distinguish, or the wrong instrument was used for one or more scales.
3. Pattern Completeness & Composition 90 pts total
61 – 90 pts
All twelve drawings are complete. Each board includes the full underlying grid structure and its corresponding pattern, properly contained within drawn margins. No drawings are missing or incomplete.
31 – 60 pts
All or nearly all drawings are present. One drawing may be incomplete or partially missing its grid structure, but the overall submission demonstrates a full attempt.
0 – 30 pts
Multiple drawings are missing, incomplete, or lack the underlying grid structure. The submission does not represent a full attempt at both parts of the assignment.
4. Scale Accuracy & Legibility 90 pts total
61 – 90 pts
All scales are accurately measured and applied using the architect’s scale. The 1/2″, 1/4″, and 1/8″ drawings are proportionally correct reductions of the Part 1 originals and remain legible at their respective sizes.
31 – 60 pts
Scale is mostly accurate. Minor measurement errors are present in one or two drawings but do not significantly distort proportional relationships. Drawings remain generally legible.
0 – 30 pts
Scale is inaccurate in multiple drawings. Proportional relationships between scales are not clearly maintained, or the architect’s scale does not appear to have been used correctly.
5. Graphic Quality & Professional Standards 60 pts total
41 – 60 pts
All patterns feature crisp, dark, clean lines and a complete, consistent 45° diagonal hatch. Boards are clean and free of smudges or erasing marks. The submission presents as a professional technical drawing.
21 – 40 pts
Most patterns are clean and well-executed. Minor smudging, inconsistent hatch density, or light lines are present in one or two drawings but do not substantially detract from the overall presentation.
0 – 20 pts
Multiple drawings show smudging, incomplete hatch fills, light or broken lines, or visible erasing damage. The submission does not yet meet professional graphic standards.
