Section 01

What This Course Is About

CTD 121 introduces the interior design profession through a sequence of drawing, modeling, and analytical assignments structured around the design process. You will develop foundational graphic communication skills—manual drafting, orthographic projection, scale drawing, and physical model making—while building fluency in the spatial and theoretical concepts that underpin professional practice.

The studio is structured to build skills progressively. Early assignments establish manual technique and spatial reasoning: how to hold a line, how to read a scale, how to project a three-dimensional object onto a flat page. Later assignments apply those skills to programming, plan analysis, and physical model making, culminating in a residential synthesis project that asks you to design for real people whose needs you have researched yourself.

A Studio, Not a Lecture Hall

Design requires muscle memory, spatial logic, and empathy—none of which can be developed by working from a PDF at home. These skills are acquired through making, failing, and refining in the studio, alongside peers and the instructor.

Every assignment in this course asks you to produce something physical: a drawn line, a built model, a measured object, a conversation with a real client.

Section 02

Pedagogical Approach

The course is built on foundational pillars that recur across every assignment, regardless of medium—pencil, plasticine, foam board, or conversation.

Pillar 01

Hand Before Software

Every assignment is produced with drafting instruments, pencils, and physical materials before any digital tool is introduced. Manual discipline—line weight, lettering, scale—builds the spatial reasoning that later supports any software you will eventually use professionally.

Pillar 02

Process Over Product

Nearly every assignment requires progressive documentation—photographs uploaded to a shared course board as the work develops, not only at submission. Rubrics reward visible iteration, corrected mistakes, and genuine development over a polished final image with no history.

Pillar 03

Working Forward and Backward

You will draw a plan and build a model from it (forward), and you will trace an existing plan to recover the bubble diagrams and matrices that generated it (reverse engineering). Both directions teach the same graphic code from opposite ends.

Pillar 04

The Human Body as the Metric

From the architect’s scale to anthropometric furniture studies to a scaled human figure staged inside a model, every assignment ties drawn and built form back to the dimensions and behavior of the people who will occupy it.

Pillar 05

Real Clients, Real Constraints

The Final Project replaces a hypothetical brief with two real friends, real conversations, and real feedback. Every spatial decision must be traceable back to something learned directly from the people who will live in the space.

Pillar 06

Continuous Observation

Running underneath every formal assignment is the semester-long Autonomous Sketching requirement—a standing habit of carrying a sketchbook everywhere, because design does not only happen in the studio during class hours.

Section 03

Student Learning Outcomes

Students will be able to do the following, each tied to specific skills and competencies, and demonstrated through specific assignments.

  • SLO 01 Critical Context & Professional Awareness — Critique contemporary, global, and professional issues shaping the built environment through structured debate and reflective drawing. Demonstrated in: The Dogville Initiative (A05).
  • SLO 02 Human-Centered Research & Diversity — Apply human-centered design methodologies to research and synthesize the needs of diverse user profiles, utilizing reliable anthropometric data to create inclusive residential environments. Demonstrated in: The Residential Menu (A04).
  • SLO 03 Spatial Theory & Programming — Apply design theories and space-planning techniques to organize residential programs, using abstract diagrams—bubble diagrams and adjacency matrices—to resolve complex spatial relationships. Demonstrated in: The Dogville Initiative (A05) and Reverse Engineering (A06).
  • SLO 04 Multidimensional Visualization & Communication — design solutions by iterating between two-dimensional orthographic drawings and three-dimensional physical models, while demonstrating awareness in spatial coding. Demonstrated in: Orthographic Projections (A03) and the Final Project.
  • SLO 05 Technical Proficiency & Documentation — Interpret and produce standard construction documents demonstrating graphic literacy, technical scale accuracy, and adherence to professional line-weight hierarchies. Demonstrated in: Form, Shape & Scale (A01), Reverse Engineering (A06), and the Final Project.

“Interior design is, at its core, a discipline of translation. A designer receives raw human experience and translates it into built form: walls, thresholds, light, materials, and proportion.”
