Section 01

Introduction & Student Learning Outcomes

This assignment draws inspiration from Lars Von Trier’s film Dogville. You will redraw a house floor plan and exterior elevations, transferring measurements from a 1/8″ = 1′‑0″ scale to a 1/4″ = 1′‑0″ scale. You will then physically manifest this spatial arrangement through two distinct models, using the “Dogville” aesthetic and the human-scale figures and furniture models from Assignment 04.

The act of re-scaling is not merely a technical exercise. It forces you to re-read every dimension of the house—to understand each wall, window, and doorway as a measured decision, not a given condition.

The Film Synopsis — A Stage for Behavior

Dogville is a parable set in a small American mining town during the Great Depression. The film is shot entirely on a bare soundstage. Buildings are represented only by white chalk outlines on the floor. When a character opens a door, the sound effect plays, but there is no physical door.

This device makes the walls transparent, allowing the viewer to see simultaneous actions across the entire town. It transforms the “floor plan”—usually a technical drawing—into a living, breathing stage for human interaction.

Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (2001)

  • Understand Recall and explain the relationship between scale (1/8″ = 1′‑0″ and 1/4″ = 1′‑0″) and its effect on drawing precision and physical fabrication.
  • Apply Transfer measurements accurately from a 1/8″ scale floor plan and elevations to a 1/4″ scale hand-drawn floor plan, applying correct drafting techniques (line weight, door swings, fixtures) using furniture and interior design templates.
  • Analyze Differentiate between the spatial and constructional roles of the 1/8″ cardboard model and the 1/4″ elevation-to-wall model, examining how each communicates massing, enclosure, and openings differently.
  • Evaluate Critically assess spatial compositions through photographic documentation, judging vantage points and the spatial experience within physical models.
  • Create Construct two distinct physical models (1/8″ cardboard volumetric model and 1/4″ elevation-derived wall model) that accurately translate two-dimensional drawings into three-dimensional form.

CIDA / NASAD Standards

  • Design Process Iterative Process — Demonstrate an iterative design process by documenting development stages across all four phases.
  • Communication Technical Drawing — Produce hand-drafted technical drawings (floor plans and elevations) at two scales that meet professional drafting conventions, including accurate line weight, door swings, and fixture representation.
  • Spatial Composition Human Scale — Apply principles of human scale and spatial relationships, derived from anthropometric studies (Assignment 04), to evaluate and document occupiable space within physical models.
  • FF&E Furniture, Fixtures & Equipment — Accurately represent furnishings, fixtures, and circulation (door swings, clearances) at the 1/4″ scale using appropriate templates and proportional reasoning.
  • Craft & Construction Three-Dimensional Assembly — Translate two-dimensional drawings into three-dimensional spatial assemblies (walls, openings, doors, windows) with attention to material precision and structural logic.
  • Visual Communication Photographic Narrative — Communicate design narrative through photographic composition that integrates human-scale figures within constructed environments, conveying spatial story and atmosphere.

Section 03

Instructions

Phase 1 — Orientation

  1. Watch the video explanation. Before beginning any drawing or model work, watch the Assignment 05 Explanation videos posted on Panopto. The video covers the overall sequence, the source documents on Blackboard, and key decisions you will need to make throughout the phases.
  2. Follow in-class instructions. Your instructor will review the assignment structure during the Phase 1 session. Attend and take notes—in-class context clarifies the expectations for both models and the photography phase.

Phase 2 — Scale Transfer — Drafting at 1/4″ = 1′‑0″

  1. Obtain the source documents. Download the 1/8″ = 1′‑0″ floor plan and exterior elevations from Blackboard (Assignment 5 module). These are the only documents from which you may take measurements.
  2. Set up your 20″×30″ board. Using T-square and triangle rulers, plan the layout so the floor plan and all elevations fit within the board with adequate margins. Do not cut the board at this stage—it will be used again in Phase 3.
  3. Transfer and redraw at 1/4″ = 1′‑0″. Measure each wall, opening, and fixture from the 1/8″ original using the architect’s scale, then redraw at double the scale. All measurements must be transferred with precision.
  4. Apply the line-weight hierarchy. Use 0.5mm for construction lines and floor texture; 0.7mm for furniture, doors, and windows; 0.9mm for walls. Pencils must be well-sharpened throughout.
  5. Use templates for all furniture and fixtures. Draw all doors (including swing arcs), windows, furniture, and bathroom fixtures using your 1/4″ templates. Freehand representation of these elements is not acceptable.

Phase 3 — Model Construction

Model 1 — 1/8″ Scale Volumetric Model

  1. Prepare the base. Paste the provided 1/8″ scale floor plan onto an 11″×8.5″ foam board. This is the base plate of your model.
  2. Build the wall structure. Using white cardboard, construct all walls, vertical partitions, window openings, and doorways. The length and thickness of every element must match the 1/8″ floor plan exactly.
  3. Match heights to the elevations. Wall, window, and doorway heights must correspond to the dimensions shown in the elevations provided alongside the 1/8″ floor plan.

Model 2 — 1/4″ Scale Elevation-to-Wall Model

  1. Cut the elevations from your Phase 2 board. Once all drawing is complete, carefully cut each hand-drawn 1/4″ elevation panel out of the 20″×30″ foam board. Cuts must be precise so the panels read as clean wall surfaces.
  2. Rotate each elevation into a vertical wall. Stand each panel upright so it becomes a three-dimensional exterior wall, with the drawn door and window openings fully expressed.
  3. Assemble on the 1/4″ floor plan. Paste the rotated elevation walls onto the corresponding exterior walls of your 1/4″ hand-drawn floor plan, assembling the complete perimeter of the house.
Safety: always cut on a mat, draw the blade away from your hand, and cap or retract it when not in use. Replace dull blades before cutting the foam board panels—a dull blade tears rather than slices cleanly.

Phase 4 — Narrative Photography

  1. Populate the model. Use the human-scale figure and furniture models from Assignment 04 to populate the 1/4″ scale floor plan/model. Figures and furniture should be actively placed to suggest occupancy, not simply set in frame.
  2. Explore varied vantage points. Each photograph must represent a distinct perspective within the model—overhead, eye-level, room-to-room, threshold views. The ten images should collectively invite the viewer to imagine inhabiting the space.
  3. Control the background. Shoot against a clean, neutral, shadow-free surface. No external objects or distracting backgrounds should appear in any frame.
  4. Produce ten photographs in total. Compile all ten narrative images into your Blackboard PDF submission alongside the floor plan and model photographs (see Deliverables).
