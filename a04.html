Section 01

Introduction & Student Learning Outcomes

Design is a response to human need: a chair supports the spine, a dining table frames a zone for elbows, plates, and conversation. The relationship between the dimensions of the human body and the objects and spaces built to serve it is called anthropometrics, and it is the foundation of every functional interior.

In this assignment, you will build your own reference library of standard residential objects. Working individually, you will draw and model the residential sets as defined in the provided reference documents, covering four spaces: the living room, the dining room, the kitchen, and the bedroom.

Recording your process matters as much as the result: photograph your work at each meaningful stage and upload it to the course memory board, the way professionals document their thinking and decisions.

The assignment is structured in six sequential parts, moving from inventory through physical models, orthographic drawings, assembled layouts, human figure interaction, and finally progressive process documentation on the memory board.

Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (2001)

  • Remember The standard residential dimensions and clearances that govern living, dining, kitchen, and sleeping spaces.
  • Understand Anthropometrics as the relationship between the measurements of the human body and the spaces and objects designed to accommodate it.
  • Apply The architect’s scale to translate published anthropometric data into accurate 1/4″ = 1′-0″ orthographic drawings and physical models.
  • Analyze The spatial relationship between the human figure and residential furniture by identifying what each object requires.
  • Evaluate Whether a furniture arrangement adequately accommodates the human body by testing it with a scaled human figure and documenting the result.
  • Create An integrated set of scaled drawings and models that demonstrate how the human body inhabits interior space.

CIDA / NASAD Framework

  • Demonstrate An understanding of human-centered design by applying anthropometric and ergonomic principles to the dimensioning of residential furniture and spaces.
  • Communicate Three-dimensional objects through measured orthographic drawing by producing top, side, and front views at a consistent architectural scale with an appropriate line-weight hierarchy.
  • Integrate Two- and three-dimensional design fundamentals by constructing physical models that correspond dimensionally to their drawn documentation.
  • Assess The fit between the human body and the built environment by analyzing clearance, reach, and posture through the interaction of a scaled figure with furniture models.
  • Document And communicate the design process by photographing progressive stages of work and contributing them to the shared course memory board, reflecting professional standards for process transparency.

General Instructions

  1. Review the reference documents and the video tutorials provided for the assignment before beginning. The reference documents define which sets you must document; the tutorials demonstrate the building process you are expected to follow.
  2. All drawings and models are built at a scale of 1/4″ = 1′-0″. This scale does not change.
  3. Use drawing instruments and the architect’s scale for every measurement. No freehand measuring or estimation is permitted.
  4. Photograph your work at each meaningful stage and upload it to your space on the course memory board as the work develops. Process documentation uploaded only at submission will not meet this requirement.

Section 03

Instructions

Working Scale 1/4″ = 1′-0″ All Parts · No Exceptions

The assignment unfolds in six sequential parts. Begin by cataloguing the residential sets in instrument-drawn title blocks; build each piece as a scaled foam-board model; translate the models into orthographic drawings with dimensions and a schematic human figure; assemble drawings and models into four layouts; stage and photograph the human figure interacting with each set; and document every stage progressively on the course memory board.

Part 1
The
Inventory

Identifying and Recording the Residential Sets

  • Identify the sets. Your inventory is limited to the sets listed in the reference documents, which cover four residential spaces: the living room, the dining room, the kitchen, and the bed types.
  • Record your inventory. From the reference documents, list every object you will draw and model for each space. List these items along with their governing dimensions in a title block using technical lettering in the bottom-right corner of every 11″×17″ format, leaving right and bottom margins of 1/2″. One format per set, four in total.
Part 2
Physical
Models

Building to Scale in White Foam Board

  • Watch the tutorials. Follow the video tutorials on building the furniture. They demonstrate the cutting, assembly, and building conventions you are expected to use.
  • Build to scale. Using white foam board and white paper, construct each object at the working scale. Cut cleanly against a straightedge and keep the masses geometric and crisp so each piece clearly reads as its furniture volume.
  • Verify dimensions. Check each model against the architect’s scale and the reference documents before proceeding.

Safety: always cut on a mat, draw the blade away from your hand, and cap or retract it when not in use.

Part 3
The
Drawings

Orthographic Views with Figure and Dimensions

  • Set up your sheets. On 11″ × 17″ white foam boards marked with the corresponding inventories, plan the placement of your drawings using your T-square and triangle rulers.
  • Measure your models. Use the physical models built in Part 2 as the source for your drawings. Take your measurements directly from the models so the drawings and models correspond exactly.
  • Draw the orthographic views. For each object or set, draw the top, side, and front views using the T-square, triangles, and architect’s scale. Apply a clear line-weight hierarchy: 0.5mm for projection lines, 0.9mm to define each object’s geometry.
  • Add a schematic human figure. Draw a schematic human figure interacting with each set: standing beside the set for scale; seated at the dining table; seated on the sofa or in lounge chairs; and seated or lying on the bed. Draw these figures only in the front or side views — never in the top view.
  • Dimension the drawings. Add dimensions to every view. The dimensions are taken from your models and must agree with the reference documents.
Part 4
The
Layouts

Assembling Drawing + Model on a Single Format

  • Assemble four layouts. Use the same 11″ × 17″ white foam board layouts, one for each space (living room, dining room, kitchen, and bed types). Each layout presents the top, side, and front views of that space’s set, along with the physical models placed on the sheet at 1/4″ scale.
  • Confirm correspondence. Position each model directly with its drawn views so that the model footprint aligns with its top view. This side-by-side correspondence proves that your drawing and model share one accurate scale.
Part 5
Human
Figure

Staging Interaction & Photographing Each Layout

  • Use the provided figure. You will be given a plastic human figure scaled to represent a 6′-0″ person. Use it to test and demonstrate how the body occupies each space.
  • Stage the interactions. For each layout, pose the figure interacting with the furniture.
  • Photograph each layout twice. For each of the four formats, take one top-parallel photograph (shot straight down, so the drawing-to-model correspondence is legible) and one perspective photograph (at a “bird’s-eye” angle). This produces eight photographs in total.
Part 6
Process
Docs

Memory Board — Recording Work as It Develops

  • Record as you go. Throughout the assignment, photograph your work at each meaningful stage — measuring, cutting, drafting, building, and posing — and upload the images to your designated space on the course memory board. The board must show genuine progression, not only finished results.

Process documentation uploaded only at submission will not meet this requirement. Upload images progressively as each stage is completed.

Section 04

Evaluation Rubric

The assignment is evaluated across five criteria totaling 150 points. Anthropometric accuracy carries the greatest weight, reflecting the core objective of mastering scale control. Ergonomic interaction and craftsmanship reward the quality of physical and graphic work, while process documentation and digital presentation reflect professional standards for transparency and communication.

Criterion Excellent Proficient Developing
1. Anthropometric Accuracy & Scale Control 50 pts total
44 – 50 pts
Drawings and models alike hold a consistent 1/4″ = 1′-0″ scale. Object dimensions match those in the reference documents, and the top-parallel photograph shows each model aligning with its drawn views. The human figure appears at an accurate size relative to the furniture.
27 – 43 pts
Dimensions are generally correct, but there are inconsistencies between the drawings and the models, or some measurements were approximated rather than scaled precisely from the reference documents. Minor model-to-drawing misalignment.
0 – 26 pts
Scale is inconsistent or incorrect. Objects look too large or too small against the figure. Visual estimation replaced the architect’s scale, and the drawn dimensions do not match the reference documents.
2. Ergonomic Analysis & Interaction 40 pts total
35 – 40 pts
The perspective photographs pose the figure dynamically to prove each set’s size and proportions. The body visibly occupies each space: seated at the table, reclining on the sofa, lying in the bed. The interaction communicates scale through posture, not just proximity.
21 – 34 pts
The figure interacts with the furniture. The photographs show the objects but fail to convey critical proportions relative to the human scale.
0 – 20 pts
The figure is treated as decoration rather than an active user. There is no demonstration of how the body fits the furniture.
3. Volumetric Craftsmanship & Line Quality 30 pts total
26 – 30 pts
Drawings use a clear line-weight hierarchy that distinguishes objects from the construction lines. Models are clean, geometric masses that clearly represent each piece’s furniture volume.
16 – 25 pts
Drawings are legible but lack distinct line weights (monoline). Models capture the general shape but are lumpy or imprecise, and minor messiness reduces the clarity of the form.
0 – 15 pts
Drawings are messy or hard to read against the grid. Models are unrecognizable or poorly constructed, showing a lack of care for the material.
4. Process Documentation (Memory Board) 15 pts total
13 – 15 pts
The memory board gives a complete, sequenced record of the work — measuring, cutting, drafting, building, and posing. Evidence of iteration and problem-solving is clear, and images are uploaded progressively throughout the assignment.
8 – 12 pts
Most process stages are documented, but the record is sparse or out of order; it shows that the work happened but not how decisions were made.
0 – 7 pts
Process documentation is incomplete, uploaded only at submission, or shows finished results with no evidence of progressive work.
5. Digital Presentation & Format 15 pts total
13 – 15 pts
The PDF is a professional record. The eight photographs are well-lit and correctly framed — top-parallel shots truly overhead, perspective shots at a clear bird’s-eye view.
8 – 12 pts
The file is legible, but photographs are dim or oddly angled.
0 – 7 pts
Documentation is blurry, dark, or incomplete, making the work difficult to assess.
Total 150 pts 116 pts max 68 pts max

Deliverables

Deliverable 01
In Class · Physical
Four 11″ × 17″ Layouts
Bring your four foam board layouts — one per residential space — for display and in-class review. Each layout must include the hand-drawn orthographic views (top, side, and front), the physical foam board models positioned at 1/4″ scale, and the title block inventory in the bottom-right corner with 1/2″ margins. The model footprint must align with its drawn top view.
Deliverable 02
Blackboard · PDF
Layout Photographs — Single PDF
Compile the four title-block inventory lists and all eight photographs into a single PDF submitted through Blackboard. The eight photographs consist of one top-parallel shot and one perspective (bird’s-eye) shot for each of the four layouts. Top-parallel shots must be taken straight overhead; perspective shots at a clear diagonal angle. All images must be well-lit and correctly framed.
Deliverable 03
Course Memory Board · Ongoing
Process Documentation
Progressive photographs of your work, uploaded to your designated space on the course memory board throughout the assignment. The board must capture each meaningful stage: measuring, cutting, drafting, building, and posing. Uploads made only at submission will not meet this requirement. Evidence of genuine progression — including iteration and problem-solving — is required for full credit.
