Section 01

Introduction & Scenario

Interior design is, at its core, a discipline of translation. A designer receives raw human experience and translates it into built form: walls, thresholds, light, materials, and proportion.

This final project places you at the center of that translation process. You are not designing for a hypothetical client. You are designing for people you know, whose habits and preferences you can observe and question. This proximity is a gift. Good design is specific—particular to the people who will inhabit a space, shaped by their peculiarities and their rhythms, not by generic assumptions about how people live.

The Scenario

You are an interior designer who has recently won a small lottery. With your winnings, you decide to purchase a modest house and transform it into something far more meaningful than a private residence: a shared home, a hub of friendship, creativity, and community, where you and your two best friends will live together.

Your task is to thoughtfully design this shared residence so it functions not only as a place to live, but as a space that actively supports and nurtures the friendship among its three inhabitants.

The Iterative Process

The design process is iterative, not linear. You will receive feedback on your documents as Red Marks. Leave those markings as evidence of the process—do not erase or cover them. Corrections must be made in the subsequent phase. At the end, your final deliverable will reveal the full story of your design development, including Red Marks. This project is not only about the result, but about the value of reflective, nurtured design.

You Must Empathize with Your Clients

Your two friends are your primary clients and your most important research sources. Every spatial decision in your design must be traceable back to something you learned from those conversations. The quality of your listening will determine the quality of your spaces.

Ask about daily routines, sleeping habits, cooking preferences, work or study needs, social patterns, noise sensitivity, and personal style. Ask what they love and hate about where they currently live. Ask about the DOs and DON’Ts you should consider. Ask what a perfect Sunday afternoon at home looks like for them.

Section 02

Student Learning Outcomes

Bloom’s Taxonomy (2001)

  1. Remember the graphic conventions of architectural drawing, and apply them consistently across multiple floor plan iterations.
  2. Understand the relationship between user behavior, spatial need, and built form by analyzing client interviews and translating observations into a written spatial profile.
  3. Apply design process tools in sequence to generate a spatially logical residential design.
  4. Analyze the functional and social dynamics of a shared residence by evaluating how spatial organization supports the specific needs of its inhabitants.
  5. Evaluate design decisions across iterative drafts by interpreting instructor feedback, identifying design problems, and producing documented corrections in subsequent phases.
  6. Create a fully furnished, multi-scale floor plan and a freestanding foam-board presentation that communicates the complete arc of the design process, from user research to a resolved spatial layout.

CIDA / NASAD Standards

  • Design Process: Demonstrate the ability to apply a sequential, iterative design process, making decisions that are traceable back to user-specific evidence.
  • Global Context for Design: Design for real individuals whose cultural habits, social patterns, and personal preferences shape spatial requirements, developing sensitivity to the specificity of human experience as a design driver.
  • Human-Centered Design: Conduct original user research through direct conversation, synthesize findings, and use that knowledge as the functional basis for all subsequent spatial design decisions.
  • Interior Building Systems & Integration: Apply scale, proportion, wall thickness, door clearances, and circulation logic to produce a technically compliant schematic floor plan at different scales.
  • Design Communication: Communicate design intent through partial and final technical documents, reference boards, and a physical presentation structure that fully narrates the design process.
  • Collaboration: Engage real clients in a participatory validation process, modeling professional client-designer communication.

Section 04

The Design Challenge — Ten Phases

The design challenge unfolds through ten sequential phases. Each phase builds directly on the work completed in the previous one. Students who skip ahead or attempt to complete phases out of order will find that their design lacks the logical foundation necessary to make defensible decisions at later stages.

01

User Research

Use Project Format 1 · available on Blackboard

Your two friends are your clients. Before making any design decision, you must understand who they are and how they live.

  • Conduct a minimum of five text message conversations with each of your two friends. Explore lifestyle and daily habits, spatial preferences, current living frustrations, and how the three of you envision your shared life together.
  • Take screenshots of these conversations and print them. Paste the printed screenshots on Project Format 1 where indicated.
02

Client Profile

Use Project Format 1 · available on Blackboard

With your conversations in hand, your task now is to listen carefully—not just to what your friends say, but to how they say it, and to what is implied but left unspoken.

  • For each friend, write at least one comprehensive paragraph describing who they are, what they do, and how they live. Address their spatial preferences, and the materials, colors, and textures they are drawn to—and those they want no part of.
  • Write all text using technical lettering, drawing on the skills developed in Assignment 02.
  • Record all client profiles in the space indicated on Project Format 1.
03

Design Diagrams & Reference Boards

Use Final Project Format 2 · available on Blackboard

With a clear picture of your clients, you are now ready to begin translating that knowledge into spatial logic.

  • Develop the project’s Adjacency Matrix using the three-symbol key for all spaces in the program.
  • Using the matrix as a logical guide, draw the Bubble Diagram with circles proportional to each space’s relative size and role. Connect bubbles with the appropriate line-type symbols. For a review of these conventions, refer to Assignment 06.
  • Develop a Mood Board for each significant space—an image collage addressing flooring, wall treatments, ceiling concepts, and color palette. Present boards to your friends for validation and record any adjustments made in response to their feedback.
  • Place all elements on Final Project Format 2 where indicated.
●Primary Adjacency—must be next to each other.
○Secondary Adjacency—should be close or convenient.
–No Relation—proximity does not matter.

05

Schematic Floor Plan

Use Final Project Format 4 · available on Blackboard

With the Block Plan confirmed, you are ready to draw the first version of your floor plan.

  • Draw a schematic floor plan at 1/8″ = 1′-0″ scale showing all walls (6″ thick), doors (36″ for rooms and 24″ for closets and pantries), and windows.
  • Every space must display its corresponding name, dimensions, and area.
  • Place all elements on Final Project Format 4 where indicated.
  • Explain your design decision-making process using technical lettering where indicated on the format.

10

Foam Board Presentation

Use the Foam Board Templates · available on Blackboard

Mount all work on five rigid foam boards measuring 11×17 inches, using the templates provided on Blackboard and completed in the previous nine phases. Connect the boards with masking-tape hinges on the reverse side, so they fold into a freestanding display structure.

Not a Neutral Mounting Exercise

You have full creative freedom over the shape, form, and interactions of the object. The structure should stand independently and be legible to a viewer who encounters it without prior knowledge of your project.

It must function as a physical narrative of the entire design process, from the first client conversation to the final furnished floor plan.
