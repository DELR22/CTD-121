Section 01

Introduction & Student Learning Outcomes

In this assignment, you will build three prescribed three-dimensional volumes in white plasticine and then represent each one through orthographic projections — the top, front, and side views that together describe their form in two dimensions.

Orthographic projections are the foundation of technical drawing in architecture and interior design: a floor plan is a top view of a space, and an elevation or section is a front or side view.

By modeling the volumes first and then projecting them onto blank paper, you develop the spatial reasoning and drafting conventions that structure every plan, elevation, and section you will produce throughout your career as an interior designer.

Each finished model must be mounted on the same board as its drawings, so the correspondence between the object and its projection remains visible.

Bloom’s Revised Taxonomy (2001)

  • Understand Explain the principle of orthographic projection and the spatial relationship between a three-dimensional volume and its top, front, and side views.
  • Analyze Distinguish visible edges, hidden edges, and curved or angled features within each volume, and determine how each is represented across the three projected views.
  • Apply Produce technically correct orthographic drawings using a T-square, 45° triangle, and architect’s scale, applying the prescribed line-weight hierarchy.
  • Evaluate Verify the accuracy of each drawing by comparing its projected views with the physical model for dimensional accuracy and projection alignment.
  • Create Compose three foam board layouts that integrate each physical model with its aligned orthographic views, and record the working process on the shared course Memory board.

CIDA / NASAD Framework

  • Form Three-Dimensional Form & Visualization — Translate prescribed three-dimensional volumes into accurate two-dimensional orthographic representations, demonstrating foundational understanding of form, scale, proportion, and the relationship between a 3D object and its views.
  • Communicate Visual Communication & Drafting Conventions — Communicate three-dimensional form through conventional manual drafting, applying correct projection, view alignment, line-weight hierarchy, and hidden-line representation.
  • Craft Craftsmanship & Technical Execution — Model the prescribed volumes at 1:1 scale with clean, dimensionally accurate craft, demonstrating disciplined use of studio tools and materials.
  • Process Design Process Documentation — Document the iterative making-and-drawing process on the shared course Memory board, reflecting an emerging recognition of process as integral to design work.

Section 02 Materials & Instructions

Instructions

  1. Watch the instructional videos in full before beginning. They are the authoritative source for all technical specifications: the dimensions, shapes, and volumetric characteristics of the three volumes you must build; the measuring guidance; and the drafting conventions, projection method, and view alignment you are expected to follow.
  2. Model the three prescribed volumes in white plasticine at 1:1 scale, following the dimensions and characteristics shown in the videos. Use a straightedge and box cutter to cut and true the plasticine into clean, straight, dimensionally accurate forms.
  3. Measure each finished model as demonstrated in the videos to confirm it matches the prescribed dimensions before you begin drawing.
  4. Draft the three orthographic views (top, front, and side) of one model on each foam board, following the projection method and view alignment shown in the videos. One board per model.
  5. Apply the prescribed line-weight hierarchy: 0.5 mm for margins, projection, and construction lines; 0.9 mm for visible edge definition; 0.7 mm for dashed (hidden) lines.
  6. Mount the finished plasticine model on its corresponding board, alongside its three views.
  7. Photograph each board as specified in the Deliverables and upload the photographs to Blackboard.
  8. Record your working process on the shared course Memory board.

Section 03

Board Composition

Each of the three foam boards presents one prescribed volume. On every board, the top, front, and side views are projected and aligned according to the videos, and the finished plasticine model is mounted alongside its drawings so the object and its projection can be read together. The three volumes themselves are specified in the instructional videos.

The Three Boards — 11″ × 8.5″ each · One model per board

Board 01

Volume 1

An 11″ × 8.5″ foam board carrying the top, front, and side orthographic views of the first prescribed volume, projected and aligned per the videos, with the finished plasticine model mounted on the board.

Board 02

Volume 2

An 11″ × 8.5″ foam board with the three aligned orthographic views of the second prescribed volume, drawn in the prescribed line-weight hierarchy, alongside its mounted plasticine model.

Board 03

Volume 3

An 11″ × 8.5″ foam board with the three aligned orthographic views of the third prescribed volume, including hidden-line representation where required, alongside its mounted plasticine model.

Section 04

Evaluation Rubric

The assignment is evaluated across three criteria totaling 150 points. Model Fabrication and Orthographic Drawing Quality carry equal weight as the core technical requirements. Process Documentation reflects the professional expectation of recording work progressively throughout the assignment.

Criterion Excellent Proficient Developing
1. Model Fabrication & Measurement Accuracy 60 pts total
31 – 60 pts
All three volumes are built in white plasticine at 1:1 scale and match the dimensions and volumetric characteristics specified in the videos. Each model is accurate enough to be measured and projected reliably.
16 – 30 pts
All three volumes are built and generally accurate. Minor dimensional deviations or surface irregularities appear on one model but do not compromise its overall form or measurability.
0 – 15 pts
One or more volumes are inaccurate, incomplete, or roughly formed. Dimensions do not correspond to the specifications, edges are uneven or distorted, or a model is too imprecise to measure and project reliably.
2. Orthographic Drawing Quality & Completion 60 pts total
31 – 60 pts
For all three models, the top, front, and side views are correctly projected and aligned according to the method shown in the videos, with visible and hidden edges and curved or angled features accurately represented, and dimensions corresponding to the physical models at 1:1. The prescribed line-weight hierarchy is applied consistently.
16 – 30 pts
The three views are projected, aligned, and drawn correctly for most models. Minor errors appear on one board but do not undermine the correspondence between the model and the drawing. All boards are complete with models mounted.
0 – 15 pts
Projection, alignment, or convention errors appear across multiple views or boards. Views are misaligned, features are incorrectly represented or omitted, line weights are inconsistent or undifferentiated, or one or more boards are incomplete or missing the mounted model.
3. Process Documentation 30 pts total
23 – 30 pts
Process images are uploaded to the course Memory board as the assignment progresses, documenting both the modeling and drafting stages for all three boards. Images are clear, well-lit, and legible as documentation.
13 – 22 pts
Most required process stages are documented. Some process information is missing or blurry, but still legible as documentation.
0 – 12 pts
Process documentation is incomplete, uploaded only at submission, or missing important process information. Images do not adequately illustrate the progressive stages of the work.

Section 05

Deliverables

Deliverable 01 — Foam Boards
Three Board Layouts
Three foam board layouts (11″ × 8.5″), one per model. Each board contains the top, front, and side orthographic views of its model, as well as the physical plasticine model itself.
Deliverable 02 — Blackboard
Six Photographs
Two photographs per board, submitted through Blackboard. One taken from directly above with the camera parallel to the foam board — a flat, straight-on shot that records the drawings without perspective distortion — and one from a bird’s-eye (elevated, angled) view that shows the physical model in relation to its drawings.
Deliverable 03 — Memory Board
Process Documentation
Photographs of your modeling and drafting process, uploaded to the shared course Memory board as the work develops.
